This I Believe

I believe everyone should have free and easy access to knowledge.

Growing up, my mother frequently took my younger sister and me to the local public library. We'd check out endless stacks of books as well as games for our Windows 95 PC at home. I looked forward to the summer for when the annual reading challenge would start; the more we read, the more turns we had to play the summer reading board game and win prizes.

For a short while at home, I also got to enjoy cable television with the Nickelodeon wonders of SpongeBob, Doug, and Rugrats. However, after my mom watched a few episodes with me, she decided to cancel our cable and cast us into the world of PBS–and for the better. Growing up with shows Arthur, Cyberchase, and NOVA was not only entertaining, but got me excited about topics like science and math.

Looking back, I was raised in an environment where I got to take advantage of great free public services for learning, and I have a great appreciation of being able to do so.

As I grew older, the Internet became more mainstream, and with that came easy access to even more free information (although the dial-up disconnections that would occur when someone picked up the phone were not so easy). Wikipedia started to take hold over the years, and one day I found myself reading an article about one of my favorite online games and realizing that I had some information I could add. It was, after all, known as the encyclopedia that anyone can edit. I made my first Wikipedia edit in 2007 at the age of 12 by adding some gameplay details to the page. Soon after I became hooked, contributing to articles on videogames and helping revert vandal edits. It was amazing to be able to contribute to a global source of free information alongside others. It did not matter that I was just a young middle schooler; I could still find my place and positively contribute to this project.

Editing Wikipedia became a regular hobby of mine, and I expanded the ways I'd contribute. I found myself regularly uploading photos, helping new users, and reviewing new articles. When I entered college, I became involved in the offline scene by creating a student organization about Wikipedia, helping organize and attend Wikimedia conferences, and collaborating with museums and libraries to host events.

As I learned more about librarianship, I realized my passions aligned well with core library values. My work with open knowledge projects has aligned with virtually all of the American Library Association's Core values, though the one I am most passionate about is Access. Giving someone the ability to freely access information can unlock a trove of opportunity as it did for me, and I want everyone in the world to have that access. I decided to pursue a master's degree in Library and Information Science, to better understand the world of librarianship and to unlock opportunities to shift my career to focus on the LIS field.
